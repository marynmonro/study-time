Tommaso Padoa-Schioppa (born 23 July 1940 in Belluno, died 18 December 2010 in Rome [1]) - Italian economist and banker, member of the board of the European Central Bank, in 2006–2008 minister of economy and finance. One of the founders of the common European currency, the euro [2] [3].
He graduated in finance from the Bocconi University in Milan (1966), and in 1970 he obtained his master's degree from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. In the years 1966–1970 he worked in the private sector. In 1968 he was employed at the Bank of Italy. In 1979 he joined the European Commission, where he headed the Directorate-General for Economic and Financial Affairs. He then returned to the Italian central bank, and in 1984–1997 he was its deputy director general [1] [4]. At the same time, in the late 1980s, he advised the chairman of the EC, Jacques Delors [1] [2], and from 1993 to 1997 he headed the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision. In 1997–1998 he was the chairman of Consob, the Italian state regulator of the Borsa Italiana stock exchange [1].

From 1998 to 2005, he was a member of the first board of the European Central Bank [1]. At that time, he was in a group of several economists overseeing the process of introducing the euro in most European Union countries [2]. For several years he managed the Notre Europe think tank. From May 2006 to May 2008 he was a minister in the second government of Romano Prodi [5]. Towards the end of his life, he ran a private consulting company and advised the Greek prime minister [1].
